Activity 4 | Inclusive Hiring Audit

Find a real job advertisement and examine it critically. Identify what it excludes — intentionally or not — and rewrite the most problematic element to be more inclusive without changing the genuine requirements of the role. Then practise the DESC framework on a performance scenario.

1. The advertisement

Find a real job advertisement on Seek, Indeed, LinkedIn Jobs, or another job board. Choose a role relevant to your practice business's industry. Paste the title and URL — you do not need to reproduce the full text.

Two to three sentences. What does the role do, and what does the advertisement say it requires?

2. Audit — what does this advertisement exclude?

Identify two or three features of the advertisement that might narrow the candidate pool or signal that certain kinds of people need not apply. Look at required qualifications, years of experience, language, culture-signalling adjectives, and implicit expectations.

Common exclusion patterns to look for

Degree requirements that are not genuinely needed for the tasks described. Years of experience thresholds that screen out capable younger candidates. Adjectives like "rockstar," "ninja," "passionate," or "bubbly" that signal a narrow demographic. Physical requirements not relevant to the role. Salary withheld. Full-time only when the role could be performed part-time.

First Nations applicants — a specific lens (ULO4/GLO8)

As you read the advertisement, consider whether any feature would specifically disadvantage a First Nations applicant. Does the advertisement require formal qualifications that screen out people who built skills through community and vocational pathways? Does it use language that assumes a particular cultural background or communication style? Does it require a continuous employment history that disadvantages people who have taken time away for family, community, or cultural obligations? These are documented barriers — not edge cases.

3. Rewrite

Rewrite the most significant problematic element of the advertisement to make it more inclusive. Keep the genuine requirements of the role. Remove or replace only what is unnecessarily exclusive.

Two to three sentences. What does the rewrite preserve, and what does it remove — and who does it now reach that the original excluded?

4. DESC framework practice

Write a DESC script for the following scenario: a team member has been arriving 10–15 minutes late on three occasions in the past fortnight. Their work quality is good and they have been with the business for eight months. You need to address the lateness without damaging the relationship.

D — Describe

State the specific behaviour or situation objectively. No character judgements, no assumptions about motivation. What happened, specifically?

E — Express

State the impact — on the business, the team, or clients — and, where appropriate, your own response. Stick to impact and observable facts.

S — Specify

State clearly and concretely what you need to change going forward. Be specific about the standard, the timeline, and what you will do to support it.

C — Consequences

State what will happen if the behaviour changes — and, only if it is genuine and warranted, what the consequence will be if it does not. Do not threaten. Be clear.

Quality check

Does the Describe step name specific dates or instances — not a vague pattern?

Does the Express step focus on impact — not character, motivation, or assumptions?

Does the Specify step give the person a clear, actionable standard — not just "do better"?

Does the Consequences step close with clarity — not ambiguity or an empty threat?

5. Reflection

Two to three sentences. Think beyond the individual advertisement to the broader pattern it represents — including what it means for First Nations employment in this industry.
